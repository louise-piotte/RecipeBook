A. Earl Grey Mascarpone Truffles (Creamy Ganache Style)
Soft, tea‑forward white‑chocolate–mascarpone centers; optionally enrobed.

Yield: ~40 truffles (12–14 g each)

Ingredients
Earl Grey infusion & ganache base

Heavy cream 35%: 160 g

Loose‑leaf Earl Grey (or 6–8 bags): 10–12 g

Invert sugar, light honey or glucose: 25 g (improves texture & shelf life) 

Powdered gelatin: 2 g (about 1 sheet), bloomed in 12 g cold water (freeze‑thaw insurance) 

White couverture 30–35%: 320 g (e.g., Valrhona IVOIRE or similar)

Mascarpone (full‑fat), room temp: 150 g

Fine sea salt: 1 g (≈ ¼ tsp)

Optional but potent: Food‑grade bergamot extract, 2–4 drops (to boost the tea’s top notes)

Finishing

For rolling: cocoa powder or powdered sugar as needed, or

For coating: tempered dark chocolate 63–70% 400–500 g (thin shell keeps sweetness in check)

Method
Infuse the cream. Heat cream to 80–85 °C (not boiling), stir in tea, cover and steep 10–12 min. Strain, pressing leaves; weigh back to 160 g (top up with cream if needed). Using loose‑leaf yields a brighter, cleaner tea note than bags. 

Melt & emulsify. Rewarm infused cream with invert sugar to ~80 °C. Stir bloomed gelatin into the hot cream to dissolve. Place finely chopped white chocolate in a bowl and emulsify by adding hot cream in 3 additions, stirring from the center until glossy. (This is classic Valrhona ganache technique.) 

Add mascarpone. When ganache cools to 35–38 °C, whisk in mascarpone, salt, and optional bergamot. Briefly blend with an immersion blender to smooth (avoid air).

Crystallize. Spread in a shallow dish, press plastic wrap onto the surface, and chill 8–12 h at 4 °C.

Shape & finish. Scoop and roll. Either dust in cocoa or enrobe in tempered dark chocolate (seed to 31–32 °C; set at 16–18 °C). 

Freeze / thaw performance
Freezing: Place finished truffles in a single layer, then into an airtight container; freeze at ≤ –18 °C. Cocoa‑dusted versions can be frozen, but enrobed truffles resist freezer burn and odor transfer better. Professional guidance for ganache‑style fillings indicates freezing is possible, with slow thaw in the fridge at 4 °C. 

Thawing: Overnight in the fridge (still sealed), then temper to room temp 15–20 min before serving.

Caveat (mascarpone): Stand‑alone mascarpone can turn grainy after freezing due to emulsion break; building it into a high‑fat, cocoa‑butter–rich ganache with a touch of gelatin greatly reduces weeping. If you ever freeze plain mascarpone, expect some texture change. 

Why it’s a good / risky idea
Good

White chocolate is a neutral canvas—Earl Grey sings.

Mascarpone adds plush, tiramisu‑like creaminess that families love.

Invert sugar + gelatin help freeze/thaw and mouthfeel. 

Less good

Slightly sweeter profile; you’ll want a dark‑chocolate shell or bitter cocoa to balance.

Mascarpone’s emulsion is less robust than butter/cream alone (mitigated by the recipe).

B. Earl Grey Crémeux Bars (Chocolate‑Coated Custard Squares)
Custard‑style Earl Grey crémeux (crème‑anglaise + chocolate) poured in a frame, cut and enrobed.

Yield: One 20 × 20 cm / 8 × 8 in slab → 36 small bars (about 12 g each after coating)

Ingredients
Earl Grey crémeux

Heavy cream 35%: 300 g

Whole milk: 200 g

Loose‑leaf Earl Grey: 12–14 g

Egg yolks: 110 g (≈ 6 large)

Sugar: 90 g

Fine salt: 1 g

White couverture 30–35%: 300 g (for clean Earl Grey flavor), or milk chocolate 40–46% 350 g for a caramelly profile

Powdered gelatin: 6 g bloomed in 36 g cold water (stability & freeze‑thaw)

Optional: a pinch of lemon zest or a drop or two of food‑grade bergamot extract

For enrobing

Dark couverture 63–70%: 700–800 g

Cocoa butter (optional, to thin): 50–80 g

Method
Infuse dairy. Heat cream + milk to 80–85 °C. Add tea, cover, steep 10–12 min. Strain, press leaves; weigh back to 500 g total dairy.

Cook crème anglaise. Whisk yolks + sugar + salt. Temper with hot dairy; cook to 84–85 °C (coats spoon). Strain. (Professional target temp from L’École Valrhona.) 

Set & emulsify. Stir in bloomed gelatin until dissolved. Emulsify hot anglaise over finely chopped chocolate to a glossy crémeux. (Definition & technique per Valrhona.) 

Cast & chill. Pour into a lined 20×20 cm frame to 12–14 mm thickness. Chill to set, then freeze firm (2–3 h).

Cut & enrobe. Unmold, trim edges, cut squares, keep frozen while dipping. Temper dark chocolate (seed to 31–32 °C). Dip each square; set at 16–18 °C. For neat bottoms, place on guitar sheets or parchment. (Cut‑and‑coat technique from Callebaut’s framed fillings guide.) 

Freeze / thaw performance
Crémeux is essentially anglaise + chocolate; with gelatin and cocoa butter/solids it freezes well when sealed and thawed properly. Valrhona’s published crémeux and whipped/creamy textures explicitly note “freezing is possible.” 

Storage plan: Freeze coated bars airtight up to 4–6 weeks. Thaw overnight at 4 °C (still sealed), then hold 15–20 min at room temp before boxing.

Condensation watch: Keep centers frozen during dipping, then set in a cool, low‑humidity room so shells don’t bloom. Callebaut’s enrobing resources cover the workflow. 

Why it’s a good / risky idea
Good

A thin, snappy dark shell + soft Earl Grey center gives contrast and protects the custard during freeze/thaw.

The crémeux matrix (gelatin + cocoa butter) supports clean slices and stable texture after thaw. 

Less good

More steps (frame, freeze to cut, tempering).

If the coating is too thick, chocolate may overpower the Earl Grey; aim for a thin shell.

C. Classic Earl Grey Dark‑Chocolate Truffles
Straightforward tea‑infused dark ganache; roll in cocoa or enrobe.

Yield: ~45 truffles (10–12 g each)

Ingredients
Heavy cream 35%: 200 g

Loose‑leaf Earl Grey (or 5–6 bags): 8–10 g

Light honey or invert sugar: 40 g

Dark couverture: 235–250 g (use 66% for a rounder profile or 70% for more bite; both are standard in Valrhona’s truffle base)

Unsalted butter (room temp): 40–50 g

Fine sea salt: 1 g

Cocoa powder or tempered dark chocolate for finishing

Method
Infuse & heat. Warm cream to ~85 °C, add tea, cover, steep 10–12 min. Strain and weigh back to 200 g. Stir in honey and rewarm to simmer.

Make ganache. Pour hot cream over finely chopped chocolate in 2–3 additions, stirring from the center until shiny and elastic. At 35–40 °C, add butter; blend smooth. (Temps & method per Valrhona.) 

Crystallize. Spread, cover to contact, rest 2–3 h at 16–18 °C.

Shape & finish. Pipe or scoop, then roll in cocoa or enrobe in tempered chocolate.

Freeze / thaw performance
Classic ganache truffles freeze well and are frequently produced for entremets/macaron work then frozen; defrost at 4 °C before serving. 

For best post‑thaw texture, keep water activity in check: a little invert sugar/honey and a thin enrobing help. (Moisture control guidance from Callebaut.) 

Why it’s a good / risky idea
Good

Minimal ingredients; Earl Grey is unmistakable when the infusion is strong.

Cocoa‑dusted finish keeps sugar low; enrobing adds polish and shelf‑life.

Less good

Very dark chocolate (>70%) can drown out the bergamot. Stick close to 64–66% for balance unless you boost tea or add a micro‑dose of bergamot extract.

Cocoa‑dusted versions are a touch more susceptible to freezer odors than enrobed pieces.

Flavor & technique notes you might find handy
How strong to make the tea: For pronounced Earl Grey in ganache or crémeux, use 1.5–2% tea by weight of dairy (e.g., 8–10 g tea per 500 g dairy for crémeux, or per 200 g cream for ganache) and steep at 80–85 °C for ~10 minutes, not boiling, to avoid harsh tannins. Infusion guidance and practice appear across professional and home recipes; loose leaf outperforms bags for clarity. 

A tiny bergamot “lift”: If your tea’s bergamot is subtle, 1–2 drops of food‑grade bergamot extract in a 500–600 g batch will read as “more Earl Grey” without tasting artificial.

Tempering refresher: Seed to 31–32 °C for dark; keep your room 16–18 °C, low humidity, for a tidy set. 

Freeze/thaw science in dairy gels: Small amounts of gelatin in dairy desserts bind water and reduce syneresis (weeping) after freezing. That’s why it’s included in the crémeux bars. 

Packaging for gift boxes: After pieces are fully set and de‑humidified, pack in candy cups inside airtight tins or rigid boxes. If your environment is warm, include a thin insulated liner and gel ice packs for transport.

2) The refined recipe (yields one 8×8‑in slab; 49 bite‑size squares)
Designed to fill a 20×20 cm frame ~12–14 mm deep (≈520–560 g center mass). Cut a 7×7 grid for ~49 centers ~9–11 g each; finished weight ~11–13 g with a thin shell.

Earl Grey crémeux (white‑chocolate custard)
Heavy cream 35% – 150 g

Whole milk – 100 g

Loose‑leaf Earl Grey – 6–7 g (≈2% of dairy; loose leaf gives cleaner flavor than tea bags)

Sugar – 45 g

Egg yolks – 55 g (≈3 large)

Powdered gelatin (≈200 Bloom) – 3 g, bloomed in 18 g cold water

White couverture 30–35% – 150 g (neutral canvas for tea)

Fine salt – a pinch

Optional boost: food‑grade bergamot extract 1–2 drops (or FCF/bergapten‑free bergamot oil for food use; see notes)

Method

Infuse dairy. Heat cream+milk to 80–85 °C, add tea, cover, steep 10–12 min. Strain, pressing leaves; weigh back to 250 g with milk if needed (precise tea strength, no bitterness from over‑extraction). Cold‑brew infusions are smoother/less tannic; here we use hot‑steep below boiling for clarity. 

Crème anglaise. Whisk yolks+ sugar+ salt. Temper with hot infused dairy; cook slowly to 84–85 °C (185 °F) while stirring; strain. This is the classic Valrhona anglaise set point. 

Set & emulsify. Stir in bloomed gelatin to dissolve. Emulsify the hot anglaise over finely chopped white chocolate—start in the center and work outward; finish with the immersion blender (avoid bubbles). For the silkiest texture and good freeze/thaw, blend around 35–45 °C. 

Cast & chill. Line your frame (on acetate or parchment). Pour to 12–14 mm; cover surface to contact and refrigerate at least 8–12 h to set. (Resting/“crystallizing” dairy‑chocolate systems overnight gives the cleanest cut.) 

Enrobing chocolate (thin, snappy shell)
Dark couverture 63–70% – 500–600 g (choose a fluid variety or add 1–3% cocoa butter to thin) 

Cut, foot & dip

Foot (optional but helpful). After the crémeux has set cold, unmold and spread a paper‑thin layer of tempered dark chocolate on the top; set 5–10 min, then flip onto a guitar sheet so the “foot” is underneath. This stops sticking on the cutter/dipping fork. 

Cut. Trim edges; cut a 7×7 grid. A guitar cutter is fastest; a long hot knife (wipe between cuts) also works. 

Temper & dip. Temper dark chocolate (work ~31–32 °C). Keep centers near room temp—about 18–22 °C—not cold or frozen; very cold centers thicken the shell, can crack it, and encourage bloom/condensation. 

Set. Place on parchment/guitar sheets and set in a cool, low‑humidity room. Avoid fridge blasts; humidity/condensation risks sugar bloom. 

3) Ingredient substitutions & variations (keeping Earl Grey the star)
Chocolate

White couverture (base recipe): clearest tea note.

Milk 40–46%: use 175 g milk chocolate instead of 150 g white for similar firmness; expect caramelly notes.

Dark 64–66%: use 180–190 g dark and reduce sugar by 5–10 g; darker (>70%) can obscure bergamot.

Gel systems

Sheet gelatin (Gold/Platinum): 1 Gold sheet ≈ ~2 g powder @ ~200 Bloom; Platinum (~230 Bloom) is stronger—use ~0.87× the powder weight. (Bloom conversions vary—use manufacturer’s chart if available.) 

Beef/fish gelatin (halal/pescetarian): adjust with the same Bloom logic; fish gelatin often has lower Bloom, so you may need slightly more.

Vegetarian option: iota carrageenan makes soft, elastic dairy gels with good freeze–thaw stability. Start at 0.35–0.45% of total crémeux (≈2.0–2.5 g here), hydrate hot, blend in like gelatin. Texture is slightly more elastic than gelatin but very creamy with milk proteins. 

Sweeteners

You can swap 10–15 g of the sugar for glucose/invert sugar to tighten water activity and improve texture. 

Flavor boosters (use sparingly)

Bergamot extract (food‑grade): 1–2 drops in the finished crémeux at ~35–40 °C brightens the Earl Grey without tasting “perfume‑y.” Prefer FCF/bergapten‑free sources if using essential oil, and only food‑approved products. 

Liqueur option: Italicus (bergamot rosolio) at 1–2% of total mass is lovely but softens the set; add for flavor, not shelf life. (Alcohol isn’t a shelf‑life magic bullet in ganache/creams.) 

Tea choices

Choose a lively, aromatic Earl Grey (loose leaf > bags). If your blend is shy, increase tea to 7–8 g or add a micro‑dose of extract. Gentle steep (80–85 °C) reins in tannin; cold‑brew infusions are even smoother if you want to pre‑infuse the dairy overnight. 

4) Tips, tricks & pro workflow
Crystal‑clear emulsion

Pour hot anglaise onto the chocolate and emulsify from the center until elastic and glossy; finish with the immersion blender, minimizing air. This micro‑emulsion is what gives crémeux its glassy cut and creamy set (and it holds beautifully after freezing). 

Thin, elegant shells

Pick a more fluid couverture (Callebaut’s 4‑drop, etc.) or add 1–3% cocoa butter to thin; you’ll taste more tea through a thinner shell. 

Footing framed fillings

A thin “foot” under the slab keeps pieces from sticking to your fork or belt and makes dipping cleaner. 

Workroom conditions

Temper and set chocolate in a room around 18–20 °C and ≤ 50–60% RH for shine and snap; high humidity is a bloom trap. 

Avoiding bloom & cracks

Don’t dip cold/frozen centers: they thicken shells, can crack coatings, and encourage sugar bloom via condensation. Keep centers close to chocolate temp (room‑cool). 

Cut sizes that suit gift boxes

For small assortments, the 7×7 grid (49 pieces) from an 8×8 slab gives “two‑bite” bars after enrobing. For larger bonbons, cut 6×6 (36 pieces).

5) Freeze / thaw & storage plan (bonbon‑friendly)
Crémeux & crémeux‑style fillings: Valrhona explicitly notes “freezing is possible.” The emulsion + a little gelatin keep texture creamy after thaw. 

How to freeze bars: After full set and enrobing, pack air‑tight (double‑wrap or rigid container). Freeze at ≤ –18 °C for 4–6 weeks without noticeable quality loss.

Thawing: Move to 4 °C (still sealed) overnight, then bring to room temp 15–20 min before boxing/serving. Keep humidity controlled to avoid condensation (a common sugar‑bloom cause).

2) Can you use honey instead of invert sugar?
Yes. Honey behaves like an invert‑style sugar (mostly fructose + glucose with ~17–18% water), so it softens texture, boosts sweetness, and helps bind water a bit—similar to invert syrup/Trimoline. Use a mild honey so it doesn’t step on the Earl Grey. 

How to swap (for the 1× batch):

Replace 10–15 g of the granulated sugar with 12–18 g honey (equal or slightly higher weight for similar sweetness/solids).

Because honey carries water, you can reduce the milk by ~3–5 g if you want to keep the crémeux extra tidy on freeze/thaw—but at these small amounts, it’s optional.

In ganaches/creamy centers, using invert‑type sugars (invert, glucose, honey) is a standard trick to improve texture and limit free water. 

3) “Can I thin the chocolate with extra fat, like butter? I won’t be tempering.”
Short answer: Don’t use butter to thin real chocolate if you want a firm, tidy shell. Butter (milk fat + some water) will soften the set and increases the risk of fat bloom (and possible seizing from water), especially when the chocolate isn’t tempered. Major manufacturers point out that incompatible fats or poor temper are prime bloom triggers. 

Better ways to get a thinner, snappier coating:

Add cocoa butter (1–3%) to real chocolate and temper it. This is the industry‑standard way to increase fluidity and keep shells thin. (Mycryo is just pre‑crystallized cocoa butter used for easy tempering.) 

Use a compound/coating chocolate (a.k.a. “coating” or “compound”) instead of real chocolate. These are formulated with vegetable fats and do not require tempering; they set firm without the temper curve. Flavor is different, but for a non‑tempered workflow they’re the reliable choice. 

If you absolutely refuse to temper and still want a dip: choose a good dark compound coating. If it feels thick, you can adjust with a small amount of paramount crystals (palm‑kernel‑oil flakes designed to thin confectionery coatings). Avoid butter. 

Why not just add oil or butter to real chocolate?
Blending other fats into chocolate leads to a softer, quick‑to‑melt coating and greater bloom susceptibility; manufacturers caution against it for finishing work. If you do it anyway, keep the pieces refrigerated/frozen until serving and expect a dull, less stable finish. 

4) If you won’t temper, here’s a practical finish plan
Option A — Compound coating (recommended for no‑temper):

Melt to package guidance (often ~40–45 °C), dip, and set in a cool, dry room. Done. (No temper curve.) 

Option B — No shell:

Cast, chill overnight, cut, and dust lightly with a mix of cocoa powder + finely sifted Earl Grey (or superfine sugar infused with Earl Grey). Texture is elegant, but protection is lower than a shell—pack airtight and freeze promptly.

Note on bloom: Any untempered real chocolate will be prone to fat or sugar bloom over time—especially across freeze/thaw or humidity swings—so if presentation matters in gift boxes, go compound coating or bite the bullet and do a simple temper.
